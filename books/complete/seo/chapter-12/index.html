    1. Introduction¶

    E-E-A-T stands for Experience, Expertise, Authoritativeness, and Trustworthiness. It is a framework used by Google's Search Quality Rater Guidelines to evaluate the overall quality and credibility of content.

    Although E-E-A-T is not a direct ranking factor, websites that consistently demonstrate these qualities are more likely to earn trust from users and perform well in search results.

    2. What is E-E-A-T?¶

    E-E-A-T is a rubric human quality raters use to assess content and site credibility as part of Google's search quality evaluation process — not a score computed and applied algorithmically to every page, but a framework that shapes the training data and guidelines behind ranking systems, particularly the Helpful Content System (Chapter 5, Section 15) and updates targeting YMYL content.

    3. Experience¶

    Experience asks whether the content creator has genuine, first-hand experience with the topic — having actually used the product, visited the place, or lived the situation being described, rather than only synthesizing other sources. Original photos, specific first-hand details, and personal case studies are the clearest signals of experience.

    4. Expertise¶

    Expertise asks whether the content creator has the necessary knowledge or skill for the topic. For everyday topics, expertise can come from everyday life experience; for YMYL topics (health, finance, legal, safety), Google's guidelines expect formal expertise or credentials appropriate to the subject.

    5. Authoritativeness¶

    Authoritativeness asks whether the content creator or website is a recognized, go-to source for the topic — reflected in citations, mentions, and links from other authoritative sources in the field, and in a consistent, disambiguated entity presence (Chapter 10).

    6. Trustworthiness¶

    Trustworthiness — the most heavily weighted of the four, per Google's own guidelines — asks whether a page's information is accurate, honest, safe, and reliable, and whether the site itself is safe to interact with (secure checkout, transparent business practices, genuine reviews).

    7. Why E-E-A-T Matters¶

    Strong E-E-A-T helps to:

    • Build user trust
    • Improve brand credibility
    • Support higher-quality content
    • Increase authority within a topic
    • Strengthen visibility in AI-powered search
    • Improve long-term SEO performance

    8. Google's Quality Rater Guidelines¶

    Google's Search Quality Rater Guidelines are a public document used to train human raters who evaluate search result quality — not a direct ranking algorithm, but the basis against which Google's ranking systems and updates are validated. The guidelines place particular emphasis on YMYL content, where poor-quality or untrustworthy information could cause real harm.

    9. Author Profiles¶

    A visible, specific author byline — ideally linked to an author profile page describing relevant credentials or experience — is one of the most direct ways to demonstrate expertise and make it attributable to an identifiable person, reinforced with Person schema (Chapter 10, Section 16).

    10. About Pages¶

    An About page establishing who runs a site, its mission, and its relevant credentials is a basic trust and authoritativeness signal — its absence, or a vague/generic one, is a common gap on sites otherwise producing decent content.

    11. Contact Information¶

    Easily findable contact information (email, phone, physical address where relevant) signals a business is genuine and accountable — its absence is a common trust red flag, particularly for e-commerce or YMYL sites.

    12. Editorial Policies¶

    A visible editorial policy — describing how content is researched, fact-checked, and reviewed, and how corrections are handled — reinforces trustworthiness, especially for publishers and content-heavy sites where readers can't otherwise verify the process behind what they're reading.

    13. Sources and Citations¶

    Citing sources for factual claims, especially statistics and YMYL information, lets readers (and search engines) verify accuracy independently rather than taking a claim purely on the author's authority. See E-E-A-T Prompts for drafting prompts to strengthen citations in a draft.

    14. Content Accuracy¶

    Content should be factually correct and kept that way — inaccurate information, even if unintentional, directly undermines trustworthiness and is exactly what Google's quality systems are designed to demote, particularly for YMYL topics.

    15. User Reviews¶

    Genuine, unfiltered user reviews (both positive and negative) build trust more effectively than a curated, all-positive selection — and manipulated or incentivized reviews are both a Google guidelines violation and a fast way to lose reader trust once discovered.

    16. Brand Reputation¶

    Reputation earned off-site — press mentions, third-party reviews, industry recognition — feeds into authoritativeness independent of what a site says about itself, since it reflects external validation rather than self-reported claims.

    17. Website Security¶

    HTTPS sitewide, secure checkout, and generally sound technical security practices are baseline trustworthiness signals — their absence (mixed content warnings, insecure forms) actively damages user trust and can trigger browser-level warnings that drive visitors away regardless of content quality.

    18. Transparent Business Information¶

    Clearly stated business details — who owns the site, terms of service, return/refund policies, pricing — support trustworthiness, particularly for e-commerce and YMYL sites where a reader needs confidence before transacting or acting on advice.

    19. Content Freshness¶

    Keeping content accurate and current — updated statistics, current pricing, corrected outdated claims — supports trustworthiness, but only when the update is genuine. Artificially bumping a "last updated" date without real content changes is both detectable and counterproductive. See Chapter 5, Section 13.

    20. AI-Generated Content¶

    Google's guidance focuses on whether content is genuinely helpful, accurate, and demonstrates E-E-A-T — regardless of whether AI assisted in producing it — not on banning AI-assisted content outright. Mass-produced, unedited AI content lacking genuine human review or first-hand insight is the actual risk, since it typically fails the experience and trustworthiness dimensions specifically. See Chapter 9, Section 24.

    21. E-E-A-T for AI Search¶

    AI answer engines weigh similar trust signals when deciding what to cite — explicit authorship, visible dates, and matching structured data reinforce that a passage's claims come from an identifiable, accountable, current source. See AEO Book, Chapter 7, which cross-references E-E-A-T directly as a citability trust signal.

    22. Measuring Trust Signals¶

    E-E-A-T itself isn't directly measurable via a single metric, but proxies exist: author bio completeness, citation density on factual claims, review volume/sentiment, and — indirectly — engagement metrics and organic performance on YMYL content specifically, where trust gaps show up most clearly in underperformance.

    23. Common Mistakes¶

    • Publishing YMYL content with no identifiable, credentialed author
    • Treating E-E-A-T as a checklist to satisfy superficially rather than genuine practice
    • Incentivizing or fabricating reviews and testimonials
    • Letting published dates go stale without genuine content updates, or artificially bumping them without real changes
    • No visible way for a reader to contact the business or verify who's behind the content

    24. Best Practices¶

    • Attribute content to real, identifiable authors with genuine relevant expertise, especially for YMYL topics
    • Cite sources for factual claims, particularly statistics
    • Keep contact information, business details, and policies easily findable
    • Maintain HTTPS and sound technical security sitewide
    • Review AI-assisted content for genuine accuracy and first-hand insight before publishing, not just grammar

    25. Checklist¶

    • [ ] Author bylines present on content where expertise matters, linked to a real bio page
    • [ ] Sources cited for factual claims, especially statistics and YMYL content
    • [ ] Contact information, business details, and policies easily findable
    • [ ] HTTPS enforced sitewide, no mixed content
    • [ ] Reviews and testimonials genuine, not fabricated or incentivized
    • [ ] Publication/update dates visible and accurate, not artificially bumped

    Summary¶

    E-E-A-T isn't a ranking factor to game — it's a description of what genuinely credible, trustworthy content looks like, used to train the systems that increasingly separate helpful content from content merely optimized to look helpful. Demonstrating real experience and expertise, earning genuine authority, and operating with visible trustworthiness are what E-E-A-T ultimately asks for, and they matter as much for AI answer engine citation as for traditional ranking.

    Learning Outcomes¶

    After completing this chapter, you will understand:

    • The four pillars of E-E-A-T
    • How to demonstrate experience and expertise
    • Ways to build authority and trust
    • E-E-A-T best practices
    • Applying E-E-A-T to AI search optimization
